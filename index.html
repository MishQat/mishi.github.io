Autonomous Poker Dealer

Computer Vision ‚Ä¢ AI ‚Ä¢ Robotics Integration

My Role & Perspective

My contribution to this project was primarily focused on the mechanical design of the robotic arm. I joined the team at a relatively late stage, at a point where several key hardware components had already been ordered based on an earlier design direction.

The original plan was to construct the arm largely from pre-made components. However, midway through the project, the design scope shifted toward a different arm configuration. At that point, I was tasked with designing a new mechanical layout using components that had been selected for a different concept, with limited briefing on the final intended architecture.

Design Constraints & Challenges

This meant working under unusually rigid constraints: I had to use the available motors, servos, and structural components while simultaneously imagining and defining how the arm should function geometrically and spatially. One of the key requirements was ensuring that the arm could reach across the full width of a poker table to deal cards reliably.

To satisfy the reach requirement, the arm geometry necessarily became long and extended. While this met the workspace objective, it introduced a significant mechanical mismatch: the distal servo motors were too large and too heavy relative to their function, and the mass distribution placed excessive load on the base joint.

Why the System Failed

Although the arm could theoretically reach the required workspace when fully extended, the base servo was unable to support the combined weight and moment generated by the upstream motors and arm structure. In practice, the torque requirements exceeded what the base actuator could reliably deliver.

In hindsight, the design failure was not caused by a single poor engineering decision, but by a system-level mismatch between actuator selection, mass distribution, and kinematic requirements. The servo motors did not need to be as bulky at the end effector, and a lighter, more hierarchical actuation strategy would have been more appropriate.

Reflection

From my perspective, the core issue was ultimately one of project management and scope alignment. There was a substantial disconnect between what components had been procured, what the final system was expected to do, and how those expectations evolved over time.

This experience reinforced an important engineering lesson: even strong subsystem design cannot compensate for unclear requirements and late-stage scope changes. Mechanical systems, in particular, are far less forgiving to iteration than software or perception pipelines.

DMT Gravity-Propelled Car

Mechanical Engineering ‚Ä¢ Design, Manufacture & Test

Project Context

This project was a first-year Design‚ÄìMake‚ÄìTest (DMT) coursework assignment focused on designing and building a small gravity-propelled vehicle capable of travelling the full length of a 5 m inclined track using only the energy from a falling mass.

The brief intentionally imposed strict constraints on materials, components, and cost, closely mirroring real-world engineering scenarios where performance must be balanced against manufacturability and resource limitations.

My Role & Contributions

My primary responsibility within the group was the mechanical design and CAD modelling of the vehicle. This included designing the plywood chassis, assembling the drivetrain using pre-specified components, and producing detailed engineering drawings to support manufacture and assembly.

Although many components were industry-standard and pre-selected, the challenge lay in accurately modelling these parts from catalogue dimensions and integrating them into a coherent, mechanically sound assembly.

Engineering Challenges

The core engineering problem was converting gravitational potential energy into forward motion as efficiently as possible while minimising energy losses due to friction and wheel slip. This required careful consideration of gearbox ratios, wheel dimensions, axle loading, and overall mass distribution.

A key constraint was that once the plywood chassis was cut, its geometry could not be modified. This forced careful upfront design decisions regarding component placement and structural stability.

Analytical Approach

To guide design decisions, theoretical energy balance equations were used to estimate the vehicle‚Äôs final velocity. I implemented these calculations in a Python script, allowing rapid iteration over wheel sizes and gearbox ratios to identify suitable configurations before committing to manufacture.

Reflection & Key Lessons

While the project was relatively simple in scope, it highlighted how even small mechanical systems are governed by trade-offs between performance, robustness, and practicality. The experience reinforced the value of analytical modelling as a decision-making tool rather than a purely academic exercise.

Most importantly, this project served as an introduction to structured engineering thinking: defining constraints, justifying design choices, and accepting that optimal solutions often emerge from compromise rather than idealised theory.

3-Axis CNC Positioning System ‚Äì Mechanical Design Project

Project Context

This project was a second-year mechanical engineering coursework assignment involving the complete mechanical design of a three-axis CNC positioning system intended for laser-based surface heat treatment of a cylindrical steel workpiece.

The design brief imposed stringent performance requirements, particularly a maximum allowable elastic deflection of ¬±5 ¬µm per axis, placing stiffness rather than strength at the centre of the design process.

My Role & Contributions

Within the group, my contributions focused on structural design, component selection, and analytical validation. I was responsible for key load analyses, stiffness calculations, and the CAD modelling of several subsystems, including structural brackets and motion components.

I also contributed to the production of detailed drawings and technical justification included in the final report, ensuring that design decisions were traceable and defensible.

Engineering Challenges

Unlike strength-governed designs, this system was dominated by elastic deformation limits. Even small geometric changes could result in deflections exceeding the permissible range, requiring careful iteration of cross-sections, spans, and support locations.

Component selection further constrained the design: linear guides, lead screws, motors, and bearings had to be chosen from real catalogues, forcing trade-offs between stiffness, cost, availability, and dynamic performance.

Design Approach

The design process relied on simplified analytical models such as beam bending and plate deflection equations to guide early decisions before refinement in CAD. This approach enabled rapid elimination of unviable configurations prior to detailed modelling.

As the design evolved, changes in one axis often propagated through the entire system, highlighting the interdependence inherent in multi-axis mechanical assemblies.

Reflection & Key Lessons

This project provided a realistic introduction to stiffness-limited mechanical design and demonstrated how tight performance tolerances can dominate system architecture. It also reinforced the importance of early analytical reasoning to reduce unnecessary iteration at later stages.

Beyond technical skills, the project highlighted the value of clear documentation and structured justification when working within a team. The experience closely resembled early-stage industrial design work rather than a purely academic exercise.

24-Hour Robotics Hackathon

This project was a high-intensity, 24-hour robotics hackathon where the task was to design and build a robot capable of transporting a sensor-equipped ‚Äútoxic waste‚Äù barrel across an obstacle course under time pressure.

The event emphasized rapid prototyping, teamwork, and pragmatic engineering decisions in a fast-paced environment. Our team had to ideate, fabricate, test, and iterate within a single day.

My Role & Contributions

I contributed directly to both the mechanical and systems aspects of the robot: I designed and assembled a stable DC-motor chassis, integrated an Arduino Uno with Bluetooth control for remote actuation, and developed a servo-powered claw and lifting mechanism using custom SolidWorks parts.

The mechanical subsystems ‚Äî particularly the claw and elevator ‚Äî were designed to handle variable loads with minimal complexity, balancing robustness and rapid manufacturability.

Action & Implementation

  • Constructed a reliable mobile base using DC motors and lightweight structural materials to navigate the obstacle course.
  • Programmed wireless control using an Arduino Uno and Bluetooth module, enabling responsive remote operation within the course constraints.
  • Designed and fabricated servo-actuated claws and an elevator system in SolidWorks, iterating quickly to ensure effective grasping and lifting of the mock barrel.

Result & Reflection

By the end of the 24-hour period, our team delivered a functional rover featuring an innovative claw-and-elevator system capable of picking up and transporting the target object across terrain obstacles. The project was an excellent test of multidisciplinary integration under extreme time pressure.

More than a completed robot, the event was a lesson in collaboration, rapid engineering decision-making, and resilience. Applying mechanical design principles in a constrained timeframe taught me a great deal about prioritising robustness over polish, anticipating failure modes quickly, and communicating effectively within a team.
